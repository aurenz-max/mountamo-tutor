## GAP-001: light-shadow-lab

**Priority:** HIGH — core 1st grade phenomenon with no primitive coverage

### Curriculum Need

| Subject | Subskill | Current Workaround | Why Workaround Fails |
|---------|----------|--------------------|---------------------|
| SCIENCE_G1 | SCI003-01-d (was) | Absorbed into `knowledge-check` | Quiz about shadows ≠ observing shadows. Students need to SEE the shadow move as the sun moves. |
| SCIENCE_G1 | Future SCI003-01 expansion | — | Shadow tracing is one of the most common 1st grade science activities. Every classroom does it. We should too. |

**Standards:** NGSS 1-ESS1-1 — "Use observations of the sun, moon, and stars to describe patterns." Shadows are the primary observable evidence of sun patterns for young children.

### Pedagogical Goal

Students interact with a virtual light source (sun) and an object to observe how shadow length, direction, and shape change based on the light source's position. This is the bridge between abstract orbital models (`day-night-seasons`) and real-world observation ("go outside and check your shadow after lunch").

**Key insight:** `day-night-seasons` shows Earth rotation from space — a MODEL. `light-shadow-lab` shows what a kid SEES on the ground — the PHENOMENON. Both are needed. The model explains the phenomenon, but kids need to observe the phenomenon first (NGSS inquiry sequence).

### Proposed Design

**Core interaction:** A ground plane with an object (stick figure, flagpole, tree, building). A sun arc overhead. Student drags the sun along its arc (or uses a time slider). Shadow responds in real-time — stretching, shrinking, rotating.

**Visual style:** Side-view cross-section. Sun at top (movable along arc). Object in center. Shadow cast on ground. Cardinal direction labels (E/W). Optional: clock overlay showing time of day synced to sun position.

**Themes:**
- `playground` (K-1): Stick figure kid on a playground, colorful, simple
- `sundial` (2-3): Vertical stick (gnomon), compass rose, more precise
- `science_lab` (4-5): Multiple objects, protractor for shadow angle measurement

### Features by Grade

| Grade | Features | Focus |
|-------|----------|-------|
| K | Drag sun, watch shadow move. 2 positions (morning, noon). | "Look! Your shadow is long in the morning!" |
| 1 | Drag sun along full arc (sunrise→sunset). Shadow length + direction change. Time-of-day labels. | "Morning shadow points west, evening shadow points east" |
| 2 | Multiple objects (short tree, tall tree). Compare shadow lengths. Introduce "longer shadow = lower sun" | Proportional reasoning about shadow length |
| 3 | Add shadow angle measurement. Record shadow data at 4 time points. | Data collection and pattern recognition |
| 4-5 | Multiple light sources. Shadow overlap. Sundial calibration challenge. | Applied measurement and prediction |

### Eval Modes

| Mode | Tier | Challenge Type | Description |
|------|------|---------------|-------------|
| `observe` | 1 (β=1.5) | Exploration + MC | Drag sun to 3 positions, answer "When is the shadow shortest?" |
| `predict` | 2 (β=3.0) | Interactive prediction | Given sun at morning position, student drags shadow to predicted direction/length. Then sun animates to verify. |
| `measure` | 3 (β=4.5) | Data recording | Record shadow direction and relative length at 4 time points. Identify the pattern. |
| `apply` | 4 (β=6.0) | Reverse reasoning | Given a shadow photo (direction + length), determine time of day. "It's long and points east — what time is it?" |

### Generator Requirements

```
Input: grade, theme, evalMode, numChallenges
Output: {
  objects: [{ type: "stick_figure"|"tree"|"flagpole", height: number }],
  sunPositions: [{ time: string, altitude: number, azimuth: number }],
  challenges: [{
    instruction: string,
    sunPosition: { time, altitude, azimuth },
    correctShadow: { direction: "E"|"W"|"N", relativeLength: "short"|"medium"|"long" },
    distractors?: string[]  // for MC modes
  }]
}
```

**Schema complexity:** LOW (3-4 types, flat structure). Flash-lite safe.

### AI Tutoring Scaffold

```typescript
tutoring: {
  taskDescription: 'Student is exploring shadows with a {{theme}} setup. Sun at {{sunPosition}} ({{timeOfDay}}). Shadow pointing {{shadowDirection}}, length {{shadowLength}}. Phase: {{phase}}.',
  contextKeys: ['theme', 'sunPosition', 'timeOfDay', 'shadowDirection', 'shadowLength', 'phase', 'challengeActive'],
  scaffoldingLevels: {
    level1: '"Move the sun and watch what happens to the shadow. What do you notice?"',
    level2: '"The shadow is pointing {{shadowDirection}}. The sun is in the {{sunPosition}}. See how they are on opposite sides?"',
    level3: '"When the sun is low (morning/evening), shadows are long. When the sun is high (noon), shadows are short. The shadow always points AWAY from the sun."',
  },
  commonStruggles: [
    { pattern: 'Student thinks shadow should point toward the sun', response: '"Try holding your hand up to a flashlight. Where does the shadow go? It goes the OTHER way! Shadows always point away from the light."' },
    { pattern: 'Student does not connect shadow length to sun height', response: '"Drag the sun to the very top of the sky. Now drag it low. See how the shadow gets longer? Lower sun = longer shadow!"' },
    { pattern: 'Student confuses direction labels', response: '"Remember: the sun rises in the East (point right) and sets in the West (point left). Morning shadows point West, evening shadows point East."' },
  ],
}
```

### Implementation Notes

- D3 or canvas for shadow rendering (real-time ray projection from sun to object to ground)
- Shadow angle = `atan(objectHeight / shadowLength)`, where `shadowLength = objectHeight / tan(sunAltitude)`
- Sun arc is a semicircle from E horizon to W horizon, with altitude peaking at solar noon
- Time slider and sun drag should be synced (move one, other updates)
- Shadow color: semi-transparent dark gray, softer edges when sun is low (penumbra effect)

### Catalog Domain

`astronomy` (alongside `day-night-seasons`) or `science` (general). Recommend `astronomy` since it's part of the same NGSS standard cluster.

### Complexity Estimate

**MEDIUM.** The core rendering (sun position → shadow geometry) is straightforward trig. The challenge is making it feel tactile and responsive — smooth shadow animation as the sun drags, good visual feedback. 3-5 day build estimate including 4 eval modes.

## GAP-002: constellation-builder

**Priority:** MEDIUM — telescope-simulator is a stretch-fit for pattern recognition

### Curriculum Need

| Subject | Subskill | Current Workaround | Why Workaround Fails |
|---------|----------|--------------------|---------------------|
| SCIENCE_G1 | SCI003-04-c | `telescope-simulator` (target discovery) | Finding a labeled target ≠ recognizing a pattern. Discovery is a scavenger hunt; constellation building is spatial pattern recognition. |
| SCIENCE_G1 | SCI003-04-a, SCI003-04-b | `telescope-simulator` | Adequate for observation, but constellation building would enrich these too. |

**Standards:** NGSS 1-ESS1-1 — "Use observations of the sun, moon, and stars to describe patterns." Constellations ARE the star patterns.

### Pedagogical Goal

Students connect star dots to form known constellations, reinforcing that stars form fixed, recognizable patterns in the sky. This is the classic connect-the-dots activity but with real astronomy — kids draw the Big Dipper, Orion's Belt, Cassiopeia. The interaction builds spatial reasoning and pattern recognition simultaneously.

**Key distinction from telescope-simulator:** Telescope-simulator asks "can you FIND this thing in the sky?" (navigation). Constellation-builder asks "can you SEE the pattern in these stars?" (pattern recognition). Different cognitive task, different pedagogical value.

### Proposed Design

**Option A: New primitive `constellation-builder`**
- Full-screen star field (dark background, white/yellow dots of varying brightness)
- Student taps stars in sequence to draw lines between them
- Ghost constellation outline appears when correct stars are connected
- Modes: guided (numbered dots), free (no numbers, just star field), identify (constellation shown, name it)

**Option B: New eval mode on `telescope-simulator`**
- Add a `connect_constellation` eval mode
- Telescope viewport shows a star cluster, student draws lines between stars
- Reuses existing sky rendering and star data

**Recommendation:** Option A. The interaction model (full-screen star field, connect-the-dots) is fundamentally different from telescope-simulator's viewport+pan model. Trying to cram it into telescope-simulator would compromise both experiences.

### Features by Grade

| Grade | Features | Focus |
|-------|----------|-------|
| K | 3 constellations (Big Dipper, Orion, Cassiopeia). Numbered dots. Large stars. | "Stars make pictures!" |
| 1 | 4-5 constellations. Numbered dots for guided, unnumbered for challenge. Star brightness varies. | "Can you find and draw the Big Dipper?" |
| 2 | 6-8 constellations. Background stars (distractors). Seasonal grouping. | "These constellations appear in winter" |
| 3-5 | 10+ constellations. No numbers. Identify from mythology. Seasonal sky rotation. Star magnitude. | "Find Orion in the November sky" |

### Eval Modes

| Mode | Tier | Challenge Type | Description |
|------|------|---------------|-------------|
| `guided_trace` | 1 (β=1.5) | Connect numbered dots | Stars numbered 1-7, student taps in order to draw the Big Dipper |
| `free_connect` | 2 (β=3.0) | Pattern recognition | Star field shown with target constellation name. Student identifies and connects the correct stars (no numbers). |
| `identify` | 3 (β=4.5) | Name recognition | Constellation lines already drawn. Student selects the correct name from 3-4 options. Reverse direction. |
| `seasonal` | 4 (β=6.0) | Applied knowledge | "It's January. Which of these constellations can you see tonight?" Student selects from full sky view. |

### Generator Requirements

```
Input: grade, evalMode, numChallenges, season?
Output: {
  starField: {
    stars: [{ id, x, y, magnitude, isPartOfConstellation: boolean }],
    width: number, height: number
  },
  challenges: [{
    constellationName: string,
    constellationStars: string[],  // ordered star IDs to connect
    connectionLines: [{ from: starId, to: starId }],
    mythologyFact?: string,
    season: string,
    distractorNames?: string[]  // for identify mode
  }]
}
```

**Schema complexity:** LOW. Flash-lite safe.

### AI Tutoring Scaffold

```typescript
tutoring: {
  taskDescription: 'Student is building the {{constellationName}} constellation. Stars connected: {{connectedCount}}/{{totalStars}}. Mode: {{evalMode}}. Season: {{season}}.',
  contextKeys: ['constellationName', 'connectedCount', 'totalStars', 'evalMode', 'season', 'lastStarTapped'],
  scaffoldingLevels: {
    level1: '"Look at all those stars! Can you find a group that looks like a pattern? Start with the brightest ones."',
    level2: '"{{constellationName}} has {{totalStars}} main stars. You have found {{connectedCount}} so far. Look for a bright star near the ones you already connected."',
    level3: '"Let me help — see that bright star to the {{direction}}? That is the next one in {{constellationName}}. Tap it to connect the line!"',
  },
  commonStruggles: [
    { pattern: 'Student taps random stars without looking for the pattern', response: '"Slow down! Constellations are like connect-the-dots in the sky. Look for a shape — does any group of bright stars look like a dipper or a person?"' },
    { pattern: 'Student connects wrong stars', response: '"Good try! That star is close, but look for a slightly brighter one to the {{direction}}. The stars in {{constellationName}} are some of the brightest in this part of the sky."' },
    { pattern: 'Student completes constellation and does not explore mythology', response: '"You found {{constellationName}}! Did you know ancient people told stories about this star pattern? {{mythologyFact}}"' },
  ],
}
```

### Implementation Notes

- Canvas/SVG star field with varied dot sizes (magnitude → visual size)
- Tap-to-connect interaction: tap star A, tap star B, line drawn between them
- Correct connection: line turns gold, brief sparkle animation
- Wrong connection: line fades out with gentle "try again" feedback
- Completed constellation: full outline glows, mythology card slides in
- Background stars (non-constellation) should be dimmer but present for realism
- Seasonal rotation: rotate the star field to show different sky orientations

### Catalog Domain

`astronomy`

### Complexity Estimate

**SMALL-MEDIUM.** Star field rendering is simple (dots on dark canvas). Tap-to-connect interaction is straightforward. The main work is getting the constellation data right (star positions, connection lines) and making the visual feedback satisfying. 2-3 day build including 4 eval modes.

## GAP-003: bar-graph-builder

**Priority:** LOW — absorbed into sorting-station + knowledge-check, but cross-curricular value

### Curriculum Need

| Subject | Subskill | Current Workaround | Why Workaround Fails |
|---------|----------|--------------------|---------------------|
| SCIENCE_G1 | SCI003-05-c (was) | `sorting-station` (order by daylight) | Sorting by rank ≠ building a data visualization. Building a bar graph is a cross-curricular math+science skill (CCSS 1.MD.4). |
| MATHEMATICS_G1 | Potential data/measurement units | — | Math curriculum will need data visualization primitives for grades 1-3. |

**Standards:** CCSS 1.MD.4 — "Organize, represent, and interpret data with up to three categories." NGSS Practice 4 — "Analyzing and interpreting data."

### Pedagogical Goal

Students construct bar graphs by dragging pre-filled values into columns, then answer questions about the data. This is the simplest data visualization — perfect for 1st graders learning to represent observations as charts.

### Proposed Design

**Core interaction:** Category labels along x-axis. Student drags value bars (or stacks unit blocks) to the correct height for each category. Then answers comparison questions ("Which has the most?").

**Themes:**
- `seasons` (science): Daylight hours per season
- `animals` (science): Animal leg counts, sizes
- `favorites` (math): Favorite color/food survey data
- `weather` (science): Rainy days per month

### Eval Modes

| Mode | Tier | Challenge Type | Description |
|------|------|---------------|-------------|
| `build` | 1 (β=1.5) | Construction | Given data values, drag bars to correct heights |
| `read` | 2 (β=3.0) | Comprehension | Bar graph pre-built, answer "Which category has the most/least?" |
| `compare` | 3 (β=4.5) | Analysis | "How many more does X have than Y?" — difference reasoning |
| `collect_and_build` | 4 (β=6.0) | Full process | Given raw data (tally marks or list), build the graph from scratch |

### Implementation Notes

- This could also be a new eval mode on `graph-board` (which exists in core catalog) rather than a standalone primitive
- Check `graph-board` and `dot-plot` catalog entries before building from scratch
- Unit blocks (stackable squares) are more intuitive for K-2 than continuous bars

### Catalog Domain

`math` or `core` (cross-curricular)

### Complexity Estimate

**SMALL.** Drag-to-height interaction, fixed categories, simple comparison questions. 1-2 day build. Consider as eval mode on existing `graph-board` first.

## GAP-004: hundreds-chart

**Blocked subskills:** PTRN001-04-a, PTRN001-04-b, PTRN001-04-c
**Pedagogical goal:** Students interact with a 10x10 grid (1-100) to highlight skip-counting patterns, complete sequences, and discover vertical/horizontal/diagonal relationships in the number grid.
**Why no existing primitive fits:**
- `skip-counting-runner` — uses a linear number line, not a 2D grid. The grid topology (columns = same ones digit, rows = same tens digit) is the entire point of hundreds chart activities.
- `number-line` — 1D, can't show column/row patterns
- `pattern-builder` — token-based sequences, no grid structure

**Proposed features:**
- 10x10 number grid (1-100), cells are clickable/highlightable
- Skip-counting mode: highlight cells by 2s, 5s, 10s — see visual patterns emerge (columns, diagonals)
- Completion mode: given a partially highlighted skip sequence, click the next correct cell
- Pattern discovery mode: highlight a set of cells, student describes the rule (vertical = +10, horizontal = +1)
- Color-coded highlighting to show multiple patterns simultaneously

**Grade band:** 1-2 (skip by 2s, 5s, 10s) to 3-4 (skip by 3s, 4s, factor patterns)
**Eval modes needed:**
- `highlight_sequence` — highlight all cells in a skip-count pattern
- `complete_sequence` — click the next cell in a partially highlighted pattern
- `identify_pattern` — describe the visual pattern (column, row, diagonal)
- `find_skip_value` — given highlighted cells, determine the skip interval

**Complexity:** MEDIUM
**Catalog domain:** math
**Standards:** 1.NBT.1, 2.NBT.2 (skip counting), SMP (pattern recognition)

## Systemic Pattern: Phantom Primitives in Curriculum Authoring

**Affected:** SCI003 had 6 phantom primitives (star-pattern-viewer, matching-activity, sequencing-activity, categorization-activity, light-shadow-lab, bar-graph-builder). Likely affects other units authored without catalog validation.

**Root cause:** The authoring LLM (Gemini) invents plausible-sounding primitive names that don't exist in the catalog. The authoring service doesn't validate `target_primitive` against the real catalog.

**Fix pattern (two-layer defense):**
1. **Authoring service:** Add a post-generation validation step that checks every `target_primitive` against the real catalog. Reject any subskill with an unrecognized primitive.
2. **Authoring prompts:** Include the full list of valid primitive IDs in the `prd_context` (as we did for the SCI003 re-author). Never let the LLM freestyle primitive names.
3. **Audit skill:** Run `/curriculum-lumina-audit audit` on all subjects to catch existing phantom assignments.

**Subjects to audit:** LANGUAGE_ARTS_G1, SOCIAL_STUDIES_G1, MATHEMATICS_G1 likely have similar phantom primitive issues.
