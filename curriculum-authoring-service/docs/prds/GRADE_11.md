**Advanced coursework year.** Mathematics is typically Algebra II, which extends algebraic reasoning to polynomial, rational, exponential, logarithmic, and trigonometric functions. Science is often Physics, introducing mathematically rigorous physical science. ELA focuses on American literature and rhetoric. This year often determines college-readiness trajectory.

## Subjects and Units

### Mathematics -- Algebra II (Common Core HSN-RN, HSN-CN, HSA-SSE, HSA-APR, HSA-REI, HSF-IF, HSF-BF, HSF-TF)

**ALG101: The Real Number System**
- Explain how the definition of rational exponents follows from extending the properties of integer exponents (HSN-RN.A.1)
- Rewrite expressions involving radicals and rational exponents (HSN-RN.A.2)
- Explain why the sum or product of two rational numbers is rational, and the sum of a rational and irrational number is irrational (HSN-RN.B.3)

**ALG102: Complex Numbers**
- Know and use the definition of i (HSN-CN.A.1)
- Add, subtract, multiply, and divide complex numbers (HSN-CN.A.2, HSN-CN.A.3)
- Solve quadratic equations with complex solutions (HSN-CN.C.7)

**ALG103: Polynomials and Rational Expressions**
- Factor higher-degree polynomials using various techniques (HSA-SSE.A.2, HSA-APR.B.2)
- Know and apply the Remainder Theorem (HSA-APR.B.2)
- Know and apply the Factor Theorem (HSA-APR.B.3)
- Rewrite and simplify rational expressions (HSA-APR.D.6, HSA-APR.D.7)
- Identify zeros of polynomials and construct rough graphs (HSA-APR.B.3)

**ALG104: Equations and Inequalities**
- Solve polynomial equations by factoring and graphing (HSA-REI.B.4)
- Solve rational and radical equations, identifying extraneous solutions (HSA-REI.A.2)
- Solve systems of equations including nonlinear systems (HSA-REI.C.6, HSA-REI.C.7)
- Represent constraints by equations/inequalities and systems (HSA-REI.D.11)

**FUNC101: Advanced Functions**
- Analyze polynomial functions: end behavior, zeros, multiplicity, turning points (HSF-IF.C.7c)
- Analyze rational functions: asymptotes, domain restrictions, holes (HSF-IF.C.7d)
- Analyze exponential and logarithmic functions and their relationships (HSF-IF.C.7e, HSF-BF.B.5)
- Compare properties of functions represented in different ways (HSF-IF.C.9)
- Compose functions and find inverse functions (HSF-BF.A.1c, HSF-BF.B.4)

**FUNC102: Trigonometric Functions**
- Understand radian measure and the unit circle (HSF-TF.A.1, HSF-TF.A.2)
- Define trigonometric functions using the unit circle (HSF-TF.A.2)
- Model periodic phenomena with trigonometric functions (HSF-TF.B.5)
- Prove and apply trigonometric identities: Pythagorean identity, sum/difference formulas (HSF-TF.C.8)

**SP005: Making Inferences**
- Understand statistics as a process for making inferences about populations based on samples (HSS-IC.A.1)
- Decide if a specified model is consistent with results from a given data-generating process (HSS-IC.A.2)
- Recognize the purposes and differences of surveys, experiments, and observational studies (HSS-IC.B.3)
- Use data from surveys and experiments to estimate population means and develop margins of error (HSS-IC.B.4)
- Use data from randomized experiments to compare treatments (HSS-IC.B.5, HSS-IC.B.6)
- Understand and apply conditional probability and the rules of probability (HSS-CP.A.1-5, HSS-CP.B.6-9)

### ELA -- English III / American Literature (Common Core RL.11-12, RI.11-12, W.11-12, SL.11-12, L.11-12)

**LA001: Reading Literature**
- Determine two or more themes or central ideas and analyze their development (RL.11-12.2)
- Analyze the impact of the author's choices regarding how to develop and relate elements of a story for effect (RL.11-12.3)
- Analyze works of 18th, 19th, and early 20th century American literature (RL.11-12.9)
- Demonstrate knowledge of foundational works of American literature, including how two or more texts treat similar themes (RL.11-12.9)
- Evaluate multiple interpretations of a text grounded in textual evidence (RL.11-12.7)

**LA002: Reading Informational Text**
- Analyze seventeenth-, eighteenth-, and nineteenth-century foundational US documents (RI.11-12.9)
- Analyze the purpose and rhetorical features of seminal texts (RI.11-12.6)
- Evaluate the reasoning in seminal texts: premises, purposes, logic, and arguments (RI.11-12.8)
- Synthesize information from multiple authoritative sources on the same topic (RI.11-12.7)

**LA003: Writing**
- Write arguments with nuanced claims, thorough evidence, and anticipation of audience concerns (W.11-12.1)
- Write informative texts that maintain a formal, objective style while managing complexity (W.11-12.2)
- Write narratives using creative techniques: dialogue, pacing, description, multiple plot lines (W.11-12.3)
- Conduct sustained research with evidence integration from multiple sources (W.11-12.7, W.11-12.8)

**LA004: Speaking and Listening**
- Propel conversations by posing and responding to questions, qualifying or justifying views (SL.11-12.1c)
- Synthesize comments, claims, and evidence made on all sides of an issue (SL.11-12.1d)
- Make strategic presentations for diverse audiences (SL.11-12.4)

**LA005: Language**
- Resolve complex or contested usage issues by consulting references (L.11-12.1a)
- Apply an understanding of syntax for rhetorical effect (L.11-12.3a)
- Vary syntax for meaning, reader interest, and style (L.11-12.3a)

**LA006: Vocabulary**
- Determine or clarify meaning of specialized words using context clues (L.11-12.4a)
- Analyze the impact of specific word choices on meaning and tone (L.11-12.5, RI.11-12.4)
- Acquire and accurately use academic vocabulary across disciplines (L.11-12.6)

### Science -- Physics (NGSS HS-PS2, HS-PS3, HS-PS4)

**PHYS001: Forces and Motion**
- Analyze data to support the claim that Newton's second law describes the mathematical relationship among net force, mass, and acceleration (HS-PS2-1)
- Use mathematical representations to support the claim that total momentum is conserved (HS-PS2-2)
- Apply Newton's laws to analyze circular motion and gravitational force calculations (HS-PS2-4)
- Calculate momentum and impulse in one-dimensional collisions (HS-PS2-2, HS-PS2-3)

**PHYS002: Energy**
- Use mathematical representations to calculate kinetic and potential energy (HS-PS3-1)
- Apply the work-energy theorem to solve problems (HS-PS3-1)
- Design and evaluate devices that convert energy; apply conservation of energy (HS-PS3-3)
- Calculate and analyze power in physical systems (HS-PS3-1)

**PHYS003: Waves**
- Use mathematical representations to support claims about wave properties (HS-PS4-1)
- Evaluate claims about interference patterns, including standing waves (HS-PS4-1)
- Communicate technical information about the electromagnetic spectrum (HS-PS4-3)
- Evaluate the validity and reliability of claims about information transfer using waves (HS-PS4-5)

**PHYS004: Electricity and Magnetism**
- Apply Coulomb's law to determine forces between charged objects (HS-PS2-4)
- Analyze electric circuits using Ohm's law: series and parallel configurations (HS-PS3-1)
- Plan and conduct an investigation of the relationship between electric and magnetic fields (HS-PS2-5)
- Communicate information about electromagnetic induction and its applications (HS-PS4-5)

### Social Studies -- US History Modern / AP Options (C3 Framework: D2.Civ, D2.Eco, D2.Geo, D2.His)

**SS001: Civics**
- Analyze modern political movements and their impact on policy (D2.Civ.2.9-12)
- Evaluate contemporary constitutional issues and judicial interpretation (D2.Civ.12.9-12)
- Conduct policy analysis: proposal, stakeholders, trade-offs, outcomes (D2.Civ.13.9-12)

**SS002: Economics**
- Analyze macroeconomic concepts: GDP, inflation, unemployment and their measurement (D2.Eco.9.9-12)
- Evaluate monetary and fiscal policy tools and their effects (D2.Eco.10.9-12)
- Analyze international trade, trade policy, and their economic impacts (D2.Eco.6.9-12)

**SS003: Geography**
- Analyze global systems and interdependencies (D2.Geo.3.9-12)
- Evaluate environmental policy and its geographic implications (D2.Geo.5.9-12)
- Analyze resource geopolitics and conflict (D2.Geo.8.9-12)

**SS004: History**
- Analyze US involvement in World Wars I and II: causes, events, consequences (D2.His.1.9-12)
- Evaluate the Cold War era: containment, proxy wars, domestic effects (D2.His.3.9-12)
- Analyze the civil rights movement in depth (D2.His.5.9-12)
- Evaluate US involvement in Vietnam and its domestic/international consequences (D2.His.14.9-12)
- Analyze the modern era: post-Vietnam through contemporary (D2.His.16.9-12)

## Difficulty Calibration

- Starting difficulty: 4-6
- Ending difficulty: 7-10
- Target range for most content: 6-9
- Grade 11 has the highest average target difficulty in the secondary sequence. Trigonometric proofs, physics calculations, and rhetorical analysis all push into difficulty 9-10 at the top end.

## Graph Requirements

- Target edge density: 3.5-4.5 prerequisite edges per skill node (highest density in the secondary sequence)
- Key prerequisite chains:
  - Rational exponents (ALG101) -> Radical equations (ALG104) -> Exponential/logarithmic functions (FUNC101) -> Trigonometric functions (FUNC102) (function progression chain)
  - Complex numbers (ALG102) -> Polynomial zeros (ALG103) -> Factor/Remainder theorems -> Rational expressions (algebra chain)
  - Polynomial equations (ALG104) -> Nonlinear systems -> Advanced function analysis (FUNC101) -> Function composition and inverses
  - Newton's laws (PHYS001) -> Work-energy theorem (PHYS002) -> Conservation of energy -> Power (physics chain)
  - Coulomb's law (PHYS004) -> Electric circuits -> Ohm's law -> Electromagnetic induction (E&M chain)
  - American literature (LA001) -> Foundational documents (LA002) -> Rhetorical analysis -> Research writing (LA003) (ELA chain)
  - World Wars (SS004) -> Cold War -> Civil rights -> Vietnam -> Modern era (chronological chain)
- Cross-subject edges: trigonometric functions support physics wave analysis, statistical inference supports experimental design in physics, rhetorical analysis supports policy evaluation in social studies

## Authoring Notes

- Algebra II is the course most strongly correlated with college readiness. Ensure that function analysis (FUNC101) and trigonometric functions (FUNC102) are deeply covered, not just procedurally.
- The transition from right-triangle trigonometry (Grade 10 Geometry) to unit-circle trigonometry (Grade 11 Algebra II) is a known difficulty spike. Include bridging content that connects the two approaches explicitly.
- Physics requires Grade 11 math capabilities (algebra, trigonometry). Ensure that physics items do not require calculus, which belongs to Grade 12. Vector decomposition using trigonometry is appropriate; derivatives and integrals are not.
- American Literature (LA001) should span colonial through modern periods, not just canonical authors. Include diverse voices and perspectives.
- Statistics and probability (SP005) at this level introduces formal inference. Items should require understanding of sampling distributions, margin of error, and the logic of hypothesis testing, not just computation.
- Common Core standards referenced: HSN-RN.A.1-2, HSN-RN.B.3, HSN-CN.A.1-3, HSN-CN.C.7, HSA-SSE.A.2, HSA-APR.B.2-3, HSA-APR.D.6-7, HSA-REI.A.2, HSA-REI.B.4, HSA-REI.C.6-7, HSA-REI.D.11, HSF-IF.C.7-9, HSF-BF.A.1, HSF-BF.B.4-5, HSF-TF.A.1-2, HSF-TF.B.5, HSF-TF.C.8, HSS-IC.A.1-2, HSS-IC.B.3-6, HSS-CP.A.1-5, HSS-CP.B.6-9.
